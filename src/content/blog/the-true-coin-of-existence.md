One morning, I was sitting on a bench watching the [Sihl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sihl) flow slowly by, whilst a few fall-yellowed leaves drifted lazily along with the current. The air was still, the sky grey, and as I watched the river flow, I felt as though I were one of those leaves myself; calm, I simply watched.

I saw a [cormorant](https://www.vogelwarte.ch/de/voegel-der-schweiz/kormoran/), equally patient, drifting with the current, occasionally diving deep among the seaweed in search of something to eat. I watched as, with a swift movement of its neck, it seized a perch and swallowed it.

I was both perplexed and fascinated. I think it was the first time I had witnessed the exact moment when one life took over another.

This thought was followed by others. I thought of the effort the cormorant made to swim in the cold water, stretching its neck as it tried to catch a fish. And of the fish, trying to escape from the beak of the hungry bird by swimming faster.

Lost in thoughts like these, I let myself be carried away by them, whilst the cormorant had caught another fish and flown off elsewhere. The leaves, drifting slowly, continued to speckle the dark waters of the Sihl as they floated along in the cold of that November morning. Like them, I let my thoughts drift, struck by what I had seen.

I thought that linking the cold water of the river, the efforts of the cormorant and the fish – to catch and not to be caught – and that final flight away with a fish in its beak, there was a common thread, constantly exchanged between the parties.

I broadened my perspective to consider the bigger picture, taking a step back from what I had observed.

In nature, the fundamental biological tendency of every organism, including us humans, is to propagate its genes so that they endure over time<sup>1</sup>. We do not do this consciously, but it is a form of selective pressure: those who pass on copies of their genes most effectively leave behind more descendants. The survival of the species is not the aim, but the consequence of this process. For this reason, many multicellular organisms follow a common cycle comprising birth, growth, maturity, reproduction, ageing – and ultimately death<sup>2</sup>.

As with the small perch, for many species death in the wild is caused by predation<sup>3</sup>. The prey is, in essence, a 'currency' that enables the predator's species to survive. The cormorant does not swallow the fish out of pure malice and sadism, as if it were [Cronus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cronus), but because that fish represents the resource that will provide it with the energy necessary for its vital functions and thermoregulation. And that extra fish in its beak isn't just for the bird's own consumption, but is likely a meal for whoever is waiting in the nest.

To understand the true starting point of this chain, I thought: where does the flow of energy begin?

The sun radiates abundant radiation, the energy of which is captured by photosynthesis: a process that fixes carbon, transforming inorganic substances such as water and carbon dioxide into organic matter, namely carbohydrates<sup>4</sup>. Those carbohydrates then enter the food chain, available to various living organisms: from algae to fish, from fish to cormorants<sup>5</sup>.

Energy, I thought in very simple terms, is the real currency, constantly exchanged between every organism. Ourselves included.

Lost in thought, I let my mind wander further. I thought about the system that we humans have built and called 'society', which is founded on the very same biological principles that govern every other living thing: survival and reproduction. In our own way, just like the cormorant and the fish, we too are driven to seek out and pursue whatever keeps us alive and healthy, and ensures the survival of our genes.

I stood there, lost in these thoughts, whilst the water flowed and people continued to stroll past me, absorbed in their own anxieties, joys and daily concerns. Perhaps unaware that, in these terms, everything we do revolves around a molecule called [ATP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate). And unaware, perhaps, that the richest organism in the world is also the humblest: it does not move, it has no apparent defences, and yet it is capable of creating its own wealth by standing still, simply by existing.
